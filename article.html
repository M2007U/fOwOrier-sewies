ðŸŸ¡ 1 - What is Fourier Series ?

1.1 - Euler's Formula

Everything starts with one of the most famous equation that changes the world :

$$ e^{i\theta} = cos(\theta) + isin(\theta) $$
There are a lot of available proof of this equation, So I am going to ignore the proof here. If you need some pointers, you can try to look at the Taylor Series for $ e, cos(\theta), sin(\theta) $, or you can look from a derivative perspective and see what does $\frac{d}{d\theta}e^{i\theta}$ do.

But as a quick refresher, $e^{i\theta}$ can be treated as a unit vector pointing at a direction in the complex plane where the angle from the Positive Side of the Real Axis, counter clock wise, to the Unit Vector is $\theta$ in radiant.

1.2 - Spinning at a steady Rate

In this case we can use time $(t)$ to describe $\theta$, as $t$ increases linearly, $\theta$ also increases linearly, i.e., spinning at a steady rate. Here we can decide how fast $\theta$ is increasing, in this case we have a parameter : frequency $(f)$. We can also give $\theta$ an offset, i.e. an initial pointing direction, here we can call it phase $(\phi)$. Below is how we can describe $\theta$ :

$$ \begin{align*} \theta &= ft + \phi\\ e^{i\theta} &= e^{i(ft+\phi)} \end{align*} $$

now we can scale this spinning unit vector to some length, which we call it the amplitude $(A)$. So a single spinning vector in the complex plane can be written as follows :

$$ \begin{equation} w = Ae^{i(ft+\phi)} \end{equation} $$

if we are dealing with a lot of spining vectors, we can index them $(n)$

$$ \begin{equation} w_n = A_ne^{i(f_nt+\phi_n)} \end{equation} $$

the way that Fourier Series works is that we have some numbers of vectors $(w)$, each with a steady spinning frequency $(f)$, each starting at a direction $(\phi)$ , and each has an amplitude/magnitude of $(A)$. So if we add all the vectors $w$ tip to tail, the final vector $v$ at time $t$ will be something like this :

$$ \begin{align*} v_t &= A_0e^{i(f_0t+\phi_0)} + A_1e^{i(f_1t+\phi_1)} + A_2e^{i(f_2t+\phi_2)} + ... + A_ne^{i(f_nt+\phi_n)}\\\\ v_t &= \sum_{n=0}^{N} A_ne^{i(f_nt+\phi_n)} \end{align*} $$

1.3 - Compacting datas

However, I find that Sigma notation are a bit tiring to read and unpack, it is harder to see patterns in some cases, and some notations can be boiled down to something simpler. So below are some self made notations that are not used widely and will only work in this document

$$ \begin{align*} v_t = \begin{pmatrix} A_0e^{i(f_0t+\phi_0)}\\ A_1e^{i(f_1t+\phi_1)}\\ A_2e^{i(f_2t+\phi_2)}\\ ...\\ A_ne^{i(f_nt+\phi_n)} \end{pmatrix}^+ = \begin{pmatrix} f_0 & \phi_0 & A_0 \\ f_1 & \phi_1 & A_1 \\ f_2 & \phi_2 & A_2 \\ ... & ... & ... \\ f_n & \phi_n & A_n \\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} \end{align*} $$

ðŸŸ¡ 2 - WorldSpace VS LocalSpace

2.1 - What's the difference ?

World space means that the rotation of the radii are relative to your screen. Local space means that the rotation of a radius is relative to its previous radius
assume that we have the following configurations :

$$ \begin{align*} v_t &= \begin{pmatrix} 360 & 0 & 1\\ 0&0&1\\ 0&0&1\\ 0&0&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_Wspace}\\\\ v_t &= \begin{pmatrix} 360 & 0 & 1\\ 0&0&1\\ 0&0&1\\ 0&0&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_Lspace} \end{align*} $$

Both of them are quite similar, but both of these has extremely different results. I am aware that I am not the only one who is looking into Fourier Series, there are other creators who provide Length Ratios and Speed Ratios and share them as videos and animations.

For the world space one, $w_0$ will be the only spinning vector, $w_1, w_2, w_3$ will be pointing to the right. In this case we will end up with a circle shifted to the right.

For the local space one, $w_0$ will spin all $w_n$. You can imagine that every vector is a wiper, and the way that 2 wipers are connected is via a spinning motor. When the main motor spins and the rest of the motor stays, the whole structure spins. Tracing a big circle in the middle.

2.2.3 - Wait a minute ...

Table conversion is surly time consuming but easy to understand, but from the tables we can learn that

$$ \begin{align*} f_{world,3} &= f_{local,0} + f_{local,1} + f_{local,2} + f_{local,3}\\\\ &= f_{world,2} + f_{local,3} \end{align*} $$

or we can generalize it as follows :

$$ \begin{align*} f_{world,n} ~&=~ f_{local,0} ~+~ f_{local,1} ~+~ f_{local,2} ~+~ ... ~+~ f_{local,n}\\\\ f_{world,n} ~&=~ f_{world,n-1} + f_{local,n} \end{align*} $$

small quirk to remember is that

$$ \begin{align*} f_{world,0} \equiv f_{local,0} \end{align*} $$

ðŸŸ¡ 3 - Local Space Concepts

when dealing with LocalSpace configurations, there are some things to keep in mind.

3.1 - Congruency

In our case, if 2 LocalSpace configurations gives us the same pattern, then the both of them are congruent, or shorter, same

3.2 - Time Scaling

if we slow down or crank up all local frequencies of all spinning radii, it is the same pattern, i.e. congruent

$$ \begin{pmatrix} f_0 & \phi_0 & A_0 \\ f_1 & \phi_1 & A_1 \\ f_2 & \phi_2 & A_2 \\ ...&...&...\\ f_n & \phi_n & A_n \\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} f_0k & \phi_0 & A_0 \\ f_1k & \phi_1 & A_1 \\ f_2k & \phi_2 & A_2 \\ ...&...&...\\ f_nk & \phi_n & A_n \\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} ~,~ k \in R $$

3.3 - Space Scaling

f we scale all the amplitude by the same amount, we are just zooming in or out of the pattern, i.e. it doesn't change the pattern, i.e. congruent

$$ \begin{pmatrix} f_0 & \phi_0 & A_0 \\ f_1 & \phi_1 & A_1 \\ f_2 & \phi_2 & A_2 \\ ...&...&...\\ f_n & \phi_n & A_n \\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} f_0 & \phi_0 & A_0k \\ f_1 & \phi_1 & A_1k \\ f_2 & \phi_2 & A_2k \\ ...&...&...\\ f_n & \phi_n & A_nk \\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} ~,~ k \in R $$

3.4 - Dot Collapse

if a radius $w_z$ has an amplitude $a_z$ of 0, it can be combined with it's next radius, and the pattern will stay congruent $$

\begin{pmatrix} ...&...&...\\ f_z & p_z & 0 \\ f_n & p_n & a_n \\ ...&...&... \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} ...&...&...\\ f_z + f_n & p_z + p_n & a_n \\ ...&...&... \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

4.2 - Simplest Shape : Centered Circle

Let's start with the simplest shape that Fourier Series can trace, a simple circle : $$ \begin{pmatrix} 360&0&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$ this means that we have a spinning radius spinning at $360deg/s$ with a radius of $1$

we can also offset the circle by adding an "offset radius"

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 360&0&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 0&direction&distance\\ 360&0&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} ~,~distance \in R~,~direction \in R $$

4.3 - Flowers and Stars

to make a flower or star, we need at least 2 spinning radii. In this case,we can have the following template :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1\\ f & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

here we can try $f \in Z, a \in R$ this template can be used to make an f-petal flower. If $f=4$, then it is a 4-petal flower you can also change $a$ to determine if it is a star or a flower. here we can try $f \in Q$ let's define that

$$ f = \frac{p}{s} = \frac{petal}{step} $$

where $p$ is petal count and $s$ is step. In this case, what we meant by "step" here is
"when it is done tracing a petal, which petal will it draw next ?",
if $s=2$, then it means that "when I am done with this petal, I will draw the next 2nd petal",
if $s=n$, then it means that "when I am done with this petal, I will draw the next Nth petal"

Another way to comprehend this is $f$ determines how fast it spins relative to $w_0$, it wants to spin $p$ times faster, but not too fast, slow down by $s$, that's why it misses a few petals

here we can try $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1\\ \frac{16}{5} = 3.2 & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} 10 & 0 & 1\\ 32 & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} 5&0&1\\ 16&0&a \end{pmatrix} $$ what you will end up is a 16-petal flower, but with a 5-petal-skip

wait a minute ...

if your eyes are sharp enough, you might realize that some how $f_0 = s , f_1 = p$, and yes it can be proved :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1\\ f & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1\\ \frac{p}{s} & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} s & 0 & 1\\ p & 0 & a \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

4.6 - Arithmetic Frequency and Geometric Amplitudes

Here is what a sawtooth wave looks like :

$$ sawtooth~wave~:~ \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&\frac{1}{1}\\ 2&0&\frac{1}{2}\\ 3&0&\frac{1}{3}\\ ...&...&...\\ n&0&\frac{1}{n}\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&\frac{1}{1}\\ 1&0&\frac{1}{2}\\ 1&0&\frac{1}{3}\\ ...&...&...\\ 1&0&\frac{1}{n}\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

here the amplitudes are decreasing as the denominator is arithmetically increasing. but what if the amplitude is decreasing as the denominator is geometrically increasing ? in other words, here's an example :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&\frac{1}{1}\\ 2&0&\frac{1}{2}\\ 3&0&\frac{1}{4}\\ ...&...&...\\ n&0&\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} $$

if we don't care about the Y-position for now, we can see that the final white trail $v_t$ traces ... a circle ? *(hey, toss this into the tool and see)* I tried with other shrinking ratios, and we ended up with different circles. Let's rewrite the whole thing in a more math friendlier way.

$$ \begin{align*} A_n &= \frac{A_0}{k^n} ~,~ k \in R ~,~ 1 < k \\\\ f_n &= n+1\\\\ \phi_n &= 0 \end{align*} $$

this means that the final vector $v_t$ should look something like this :

$$ \begin{align*} v_t &= A_0e^{i(f_0t+\phi_0)} + A_1e^{i(f_1t+\phi_1)} + A_2e^{i(f_2t+\phi_2)} + ... + A_ne^{i(f_nt+\phi_n)}\\\\ &= \frac{A_0}{k^0}e^{i(1t+0)} + \frac{A_0}{k^1}e^{i(2t+0)} + \frac{A_0}{k^2}e^{i(3t+0)} + ... + \frac{A_0}{k^n}e^{i((n+1)t+0)}\\\\ &= \frac{A_0}{k^0}e^{i1t} + \frac{A_0}{k^1}e^{i2t} + \frac{A_0}{k^2}e^{i3t} + ... + \frac{A_0}{k^n}e^{i(n+1)t}\\\\ &= \frac{A_0}{k^0}e^{i0t+it} + \frac{A_0}{k^1}e^{i1t+it} + \frac{A_0}{k^2}e^{i2t+it} + ... + \frac{A_0}{k^n}e^{int+it}\\\\ &= A_0e^{it}(\frac{e^{i0t}}{k^0} + \frac{e^{i1t}}{k^1} + \frac{e^{i2t}}{k^2} + ... + \frac{e^{int}}{k^n})\\\\ \end{align*} $$ Notice that the sum in the parenthesis can be treated as a geometric series, where the first term is $\frac{e^{0it}}{k^0}$ and the ratio is $\frac{e^{it}}{k}$. in order for the sum to converge, the magnitude of the ratio must be smaller than one, that is

$$ \begin{align*}\frac{e^{it}}{k} | &< 1 \\\\ \because |e^{it}| &= 1 \\\\ \therefore |k| &> 1\end{align*} $$

so the sum would be :

$$ \begin{align*}\frac{e^{i0t}}{k^0} + \frac{e^{i1t}}{k^1} + \frac{e^{i2t}}{k^2} + ... + \frac{e^{int}}{k^n} =\frac{\frac{e^{i0t}}{k^0}}{1-\frac{e^{it}}{k}} =\frac{1}{\frac{k}{k}-\frac{e^{it}}{k}} =\frac{k}{k-e^{it}} \end{align*} $$ $$ \begin{align*} v_t &= A_0e^{it}(\frac{e^{i0t}}{k^0} + \frac{e^{i1t}}{k^1} + \frac{e^{i2t}}{k^2} + ... + \frac{e^{int}}{k^n})\\\\ &= A_0e^{it}(\frac{k}{k-e^{it}})~,~k > 1\\\\ &= \frac{A_0e^{it}k}{k-e^{it}}\\\\ \end{align*} $$

to proof that it is a circle, we will need to propose 2 things :
  • there exists a center point $c$ for the circle
  • the distance between $v_t$ and $c$ will be constant, which is the radius $r$ of the circle
let's start with finding the left most and right most point of the "circle"

$$ \begin{align*} v_0 &= \frac{A_0e^{i0}k}{k-e^{i0}} = \frac{A_0k}{k-1}\\\\ v_\pi &= \frac{A_0e^{i\pi}k}{k-e^{i\pi}} = \frac{-A_0k}{k+1} \end{align*} $$

therefore the proposed center is :

$$ \begin{align*} c &= (v_0+v_\pi) \div 2\\\\ &= ( \frac{A_0k}{k-1} + \frac{-A_0k}{k+1} ) \times \frac{1}{2}\\\\ &= ( \frac{1}{k-1} - \frac{1}{k+1} ) \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= ( \frac{k+1}{(k-1)(k+1)} - \frac{k-1}{(k+1)(k-1)} ) \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= \frac{k+1-k+1}{k^2-1} \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= \frac{A_0k}{k^2-1} \end{align*} $$

and the proposed radius is :

$$ \begin{align*} r &= (v_0-v_\pi) \div 2\\\\ &= ( \frac{A_0k}{k-1} - \frac{-A_0k}{k+1} ) \times \frac{1}{2}\\\\ &= ( \frac{1}{k-1} + \frac{1}{k+1} ) \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= ( \frac{k+1}{(k-1)(k+1)} + \frac{k-1}{(k+1)(k-1)} ) \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= \frac{k+1+k-1}{k^2-1} \times \frac{A_0k}{2}\\\\ &= \frac{A_0k^2}{k^2-1} \end{align*} $$

now we have to prove that the following is true :

$$ | v_t - c |^2 = r^2 $$

in other words, the distance between the final vector and the center point is our proposed radius squared. And here is the proof that $v_t$ traces a circle :

$$ \begin{align*} &v_t - c\\\\ &= \frac{A_0e^{it}k}{k-e^{it}} - \frac{A_0k}{k^2-1} = \frac{(A_0e^{it}k)(k^2-1) - (A_0k)(k-e^{it})}{(k-e^{it})(k^2-1)} \\\\ &= \frac{(A_0e^{it}k^3 - A_0e^{it}k) - (A_0k^2-A_0ke^{it})}{(k-e^{it})(k^2-1)} = \frac{A_0e^{it}k^3 - A_0k^2 }{(k-e^{it})(k^2-1)}\\\\ &= \frac{(A_0k^2)(e^{it}k - 1) }{(k-e^{it})(k^2-1)} = r\frac{(e^{it}k - 1) }{(k-e^{it})}\\\\ \end{align*} $$



$$ \begin{align*} &|v_t-c|^2 \\\\ &= (|r\frac{(e^{it}k - 1) }{(k-e^{it})}|)^2 = (|r|\frac{|(e^{it}k - 1)| }{|(k-e^{it})|}|)^2 = |r|^2\frac{|(e^{it}k - 1)|^2 }{|(k-e^{it})|^2} = r^2\frac{(e^{it}k-1)(e^{-it}k-1) }{(k-e^{it})(k-e^{-it})}\\\\ &= r^2 \frac{k^2 - ke^{it} - ke^{-it} + 1}{k^2 - ke^{-it} - ke^{it} + 1} = r^2 \end{align*} $$

and there we have it, QED OwO/ if these steps does not make sense at all, here are a few things you can look into, then come back :
  • complex numbers
  • complex conjugates
  • complex modulus

4.7 - LissajOwOus Curve

before diving into the fancy curve, we first have to figure out how to create sine / cosine waves :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -1&0&1\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -2&0&1\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

if we keep track of it's x-position, we can see that it is tracing a cosine wave

let's try to add another radius $w_2$ such that it keeps pointing to the right

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -1&0&1\\ \\ 0&0&4 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -2&0&1\\ \\ 1&0&4 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

in this case, $w_2$ is added for visual aiding, now the cosine wave is shifted to the right.

now we can try to let the final vector $v_t$ move in the y-direction as well by adding $w_3$ and $w_4$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -1&0&1\\ \\ 0&0&4\\ \\ 1&0&1\\ -1&180&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&1\\ -2&0&1\\ \\ 1&0&4\\ \\ 1&0&1\\ -2&180&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

here you can see that along the y-direction, $v_t$ is tracing a sine wave. And with this whole setup, we are now tracing a circle, but shifted to the right, of course we can shrink $A_2$ to be $0$ and the pattern will be centered, but for now, let's leave it as it is.

let's call $w_0 + w_1$ Oscillator-X and $w_3+w_4$ Oscillator-Y. In this case, we can let Oscillator-X and Oscillator-Y oscillate at a different rate by multiplying some coefficients to its frequency :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1X&0&1\\ -1X&0&1\\ \\ 0&0&D\\ \\ 1Y&0&1\\ -1Y&180&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1X&0&1\\ -2X&0&1\\ \\ 1X&0&D\\ \\ 1Y&0&1\\ -2Y&180&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

and offcourse we can offset the phase of Oscilator-Y as well

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1X&0&1\\ -1X&0&1\\ \\ 0&0&D\\ \\ 1Y&\phi&1\\ -1Y&180-\phi&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_WSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} 1X&0&1\\ -2X&0&1\\ \\ 1X&0&D\\ \\ 1Y&\phi&1\\ -2Y&180-2\phi&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

if you are too lazy to do some brain arithmetic yourself, we can extend some dots (refer to section 3.4)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} X&0&1\\ -2X&0&1\\ \\ X&0&D\\ \\ Y&\phi&1\\ -2Y&180-2\phi&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} X&0&1\\ -X&0&0\\ -X&0&1\\ \\ X&0&D\\ \\ Y&\phi&1\\ -Y&180&0\\ -Y&-2\phi&1 \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} X&0&1\\ -X&0&0\\ -X&0&1\\ \\ X&0&D\\ \\ Y&\phi&1\\ -Y&180&0\\ 0&-\phi&0\\ -Y&-\phi&1\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

at this point we can also control the amplitude for both Oscillator-X and Oscillator-Y

$$ \begin{pmatrix} X&0&W\\ -2X&0&W\\ \\ X&0&D\\ \\ Y&\phi&H\\ -2Y&180-2\phi&H \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} = \begin{pmatrix} X&0&W\\ -X&0&0\\ -X&0&W\\ \\ X&0&D\\ \\ Y&\phi&H\\ -Y&180&0\\ 0&-\phi&0\\ -Y&-\phi&H\\ \end{pmatrix}^{fowo\_LSpace} $$

so here's a quick reminder that
  • X : Horizontal frequency
  • Y : Vertical frequency
  • W : Horizontal Radius
  • H : Vertical radius
  • $\phi$ : Vertical phase offset
